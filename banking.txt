BANKING KNOWLEDGE BASE

Banking is the business of accepting deposits and lending money.
A bank provides financial services to individuals and businesses.
The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) is India's central bank.
RBI regulates banks in India.
A savings account is designed for personal savings.
A current account is designed for businesses and frequent transactions.
Fixed Deposits (FDs) offer a fixed interest rate for a fixed tenure.
Recurring Deposits (RDs) allow monthly investments with interest.
KYC stands for Know Your Customer.
Banks require KYC documents before opening most accounts.
Common KYC documents include Aadhaar, PAN, Passport and Driving Licence.
A debit card spends money directly from the linked bank account.
A credit card allows borrowing up to an approved credit limit.
UPI stands for Unified Payments Interface.
UPI enables instant bank-to-bank transfers.
NEFT is used for electronic fund transfers.
RTGS is generally used for high-value transfers.
IMPS provides instant 24x7 money transfers.
Internet banking allows customers to access accounts online.
Mobile banking enables banking through smartphone apps.
ATMs allow cash withdrawal, balance enquiry and mini statements.
A cheque is a written order to pay money.
A demand draft is prepaid and issued by a bank.
Interest is the amount earned on deposits or paid on loans.
EMI stands for Equated Monthly Installment.
Home loans are used to purchase residential property.
Personal loans are generally unsecured.
Education loans help finance higher education.
Gold loans are secured using gold as collateral.
Vehicle loans finance cars and two-wheelers.
MSME loans support small businesses.
Agriculture loans help farmers finance farming activities.
Crop loans are short-term agricultural loans.
Credit scores indicate repayment history and creditworthiness.
Banks use credit scores while approving loans.
Phishing is a fraudulent attempt to steal sensitive information.
Never share OTP, PIN, CVV or passwords.
Use strong passwords for internet banking.
Enable two-factor authentication whenever available.
Nomination helps transfer account funds after the account holder's death.
A locker is used to store valuables securely.
Passbooks record account transactions.
Bank statements summarize account activity.
Overdraft allows withdrawal beyond available balance under approved limits.
NPA stands for Non-Performing Asset.
CRR means Cash Reserve Ratio.
SLR means Statutory Liquidity Ratio.
Repo Rate is the rate at which RBI lends to banks.
Reverse Repo Rate is the rate at which RBI borrows from banks.
Inflation affects interest rates.
Mutual funds are market-linked investments.
SIP stands for Systematic Investment Plan.
Insurance provides financial protection against risks.
PMJDY promotes financial inclusion.
PM-KISAN provides income support to eligible farmers.
Digital payments reduce dependency on cash.
Bank reconciliation compares bank records with business records.
Customer service includes complaint handling and grievance resolution.
Always verify beneficiaries before transferring funds.
Never click unknown banking links received through SMS or email.
Regularly monitor account statements for unauthorized transactions.

COMPREHENSIVE BANKING FACTS
ORIGINS AND HISTORY
The word "bank" comes from the Italian word "banco," meaning bench, as early Italian bankers conducted business on benches in public markets.

The oldest known bank in the world is the Monte dei Paschi di Siena, founded in 1472 in Siena, Italy. It is still operating today.

Ancient Mesopotamia is believed to have invented the concept of banking, with temples and palaces serving as safe storage places for grain and other valuables.

The first coins were minted in the Kingdom of Lydia (now Turkey) around 600 BC, revolutionizing trade and banking.

In ancient Greece, temples served as banks, offering loans, deposits, and currency exchange services.

The Roman Empire had a sophisticated banking system with bankers called "argentarii" and "mensarii" who provided financial services throughout the empire.

The Bank of England was established in 1694 and is considered the second oldest central bank in the world, after Sweden's Riksbank.

The first banknotes were issued in China during the Tang Dynasty (618-907 AD) as a solution to the shortage of copper coins.

The Federal Reserve System, the central bank of the United States, was created in 1913 in response to a series of financial panics.

The Glass-Steagall Act of 1933 separated commercial banking from investment banking to protect depositors from risky investments.

This act was largely repealed in 1999 with the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act, which many economists believe contributed to the 2008 financial crisis.

The first credit card, the Diners Club Card, was introduced in 1950 by Frank McNamara after he forgot his wallet at a restaurant.

The automated teller machine (ATM) was invented in 1967 by John Shepherd-Barron, who got the idea from a chocolate vending machine.

The first ATM was installed at a Barclays Bank branch in Enfield, London, on June 27, 1967.

BANKING STATISTICS
There are approximately 4,500 commercial banks in the United States, down from over 14,000 in the 1980s due to consolidation.

Globally, there are more than 30,000 banks operating in over 200 countries.

The largest bank in the world by total assets is the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC), with over $5.5 trillion in assets.

JPMorgan Chase is the largest bank in the United States, with approximately $3.9 trillion in assets.

The global banking industry generates over $5 trillion in annual revenue.

There are over 500,000 bank branches worldwide, with China having the most at over 200,000 branches.

The total value of all currency in circulation worldwide is approximately $7.5 trillion.

Central banks worldwide hold over $12 trillion in foreign exchange reserves.

TYPES OF BANKS
Commercial banks accept deposits and make loans to individuals and businesses, and they are the most common type of bank.

Investment banks help companies raise capital, trade securities, and provide financial advisory services.

Central banks, such as the Federal Reserve, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of England, manage a country's money supply and interest rates.

Retail banks provide banking services directly to consumers, including checking accounts, savings accounts, and personal loans.

Private banks cater to high-net-worth individuals, offering personalized wealth management and financial planning services.

Online banks, also known as neobanks, operate exclusively on the internet without physical branches, offering lower fees and higher interest rates.

Credit unions are member-owned financial cooperatives that provide banking services to their members, often with better rates than traditional banks.

Merchant banks provide capital to companies in the form of equity ownership rather than loans.

Savings and loan associations, also known as thrifts, specialize in accepting savings deposits and making mortgage loans.

CENTRAL BANKING
Central banks are the ultimate source of money in an economy and are responsible for maintaining price stability and supporting economic growth.

The Federal Reserve, often called "the Fed," was established to address the problem of bank runs and provide a stable currency for the nation.

The Fed's three main functions are conducting monetary policy, supervising and regulating banks, and providing financial services.

The Federal Reserve is composed of 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks located in major cities across the United States.

The European Central Bank (ECB) was established in 1998 and manages the euro currency for 20 European Union countries.

The Bank of Japan is the central bank of Japan and was established in 1882.

The People's Bank of China is the central bank of China and is the largest holder of foreign exchange reserves in the world.

Central banks have the power to set interest rates, which is one of the most powerful tools in managing an economy.

The Federal Reserve's target inflation rate is typically 2% per year, which is considered healthy for economic growth.

Central banks can create money out of nothing through a process called quantitative easing, which was used extensively after the 2008 financial crisis.

CURRENCY AND NOTES
The U.S. dollar is the most widely used currency in international transactions, accounting for approximately 88% of all foreign exchange trades.

The euro is the second most traded currency, accounting for about 32% of all foreign exchange trades.

U.S. paper currency is 75% cotton and 25% linen, giving it its distinctive texture and durability.

The average lifespan of a $1 bill is approximately 5.8 years, while a $100 bill lasts about 15 years.

There are approximately 2.5 billion $1 bills in circulation, making it the most common denomination.

The Bureau of Engraving and Printing produces approximately 7 billion notes per year at a cost of about 6 cents per note.

The largest denomination of U.S. currency ever printed was the $100,000 bill, which featured President Woodrow Wilson and was used only for Federal Reserve transactions.

Counterfeit currency is a continuing challenge, with approximately $70 million in counterfeit U.S. bills being detected annually.

The new $100 bill introduced in 2013 features advanced security features, including a 3-D security ribbon and a color-shifting bell in the inkwell.

The Euro was introduced as a physical currency in 2002, replacing 12 national currencies.

BANKING OPERATIONS
The fractional reserve system allows banks to lend out most of the money deposited while keeping only a fraction in reserve.

Bank reserve requirements in the United States were set to zero percent in March 2020 in response to the COVID-19 pandemic.

The Federal Funds rate is the interest rate at which banks lend money to each other overnight.

The prime rate is the interest rate that commercial banks charge their most creditworthy customers.

Banks typically charge higher interest rates on loans than the rates they pay on deposits, with the difference being their profit margin.

The net interest margin (NIM) is the difference between the interest income generated by banks and the amount of interest paid out to depositors.

Banks make money through three primary sources: fees and commissions, trading and investing activities, and net interest margins.

The 2008 financial crisis was triggered by the collapse of the subprime mortgage market and resulted in over $2 trillion in losses worldwide.

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act was passed in 2010 in response to the financial crisis.

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) was created by the Dodd-Frank Act to protect consumers from predatory financial practices.

DIGITAL AND MODERN BANKING
As of 2024, approximately 75% of adults worldwide have a bank account, up from 50% in 2011.

Over 60% of bank customers regularly use mobile banking applications to manage their accounts.

Fintech (financial technology) companies have disrupted traditional banking, with companies like PayPal, Venmo, and Square revolutionizing payments.

Cryptocurrencies, such as Bitcoin (created in 2009), represent a significant challenge to traditional banking systems.

The first Bitcoin ATM was installed in Vancouver, Canada, in 2013.

Central banks are exploring digital currencies, with the Chinese digital yuan being one of the most advanced projects.

Approximately 130 countries, representing 98% of global GDP, are exploring central bank digital currencies (CBDCs).

The Bahamas was the first country to fully launch a central bank digital currency, the Sand Dollar, in 2020.

AI and machine learning are increasingly used in banking for fraud detection, credit scoring, and customer service chatbots.

Robo-advisors, which provide automated investment advice, now manage over $1 trillion in assets globally.

SAFETY AND SECURITY
The Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) was created in 1933 and insures deposits up to $250,000 per depositor, per insured bank.

The FDIC insures approximately $9 trillion in deposits across more than 4,500 banks.

Since the FDIC was established, no depositor has lost any insured funds due to a bank failure.

Bank vaults are designed to be fireproof and theft-resistant, with some weighing over 100 tons.

The security features on modern currency include microprinting, watermarks, security threads, and color-shifting ink.

Bank security has evolved to include biometric verification, including fingerprint scanning and facial recognition.

The largest bank robbery in history was the Central Bank of Iraq robbery in 2003, where approximately $1 billion was stolen.

Bank data breaches have become a significant concern, with over 40 million financial records exposed in breaches annually.

The SWIFT system, used for international bank transfers, processes approximately 40 million messages daily.

The average bank customer loses approximately $500 annually to bank fees if not careful about account management.

INTEREST RATES AND MORTGAGES
Compound interest was described by Albert Einstein as the "eighth wonder of the world," as it allows money to grow exponentially over time.

The average 30-year fixed mortgage rate in the United States has fluctuated between 2.65% (in 2021) to over 7% (in 2023).

The prime lending rate is typically about 3% higher than the Federal Funds rate.

Variable interest rates, also known as adjustable rates, can change based on market conditions and financial benchmarks.

Interest rates affect nearly every aspect of the economy, from consumer spending to business investment and housing markets.

Negative interest rates, where banks charge depositors to hold their money, have been implemented in several countries including Japan, Switzerland, and Denmark.

The Federal Reserve uses the federal funds rate as its primary monetary policy tool, adjusting it to influence economic activity.

Lower interest rates encourage borrowing and spending, which can stimulate economic growth.

Higher interest rates discourage borrowing and spending, which can help control inflation.

GLOBAL BANKING
The Bank for International Settlements (BIS) was established in 1930 and is the bank for central banks.

The World Bank was established in 1944 and provides loans to developing countries for capital projects.

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) was established in 1944 to promote international monetary cooperation.

London is considered the world's premier financial center, followed by New York, Hong Kong, and Singapore.

London became a major financial center due to its status as a center for international banking in the 17th century.

Swiss banks are renowned worldwide for their privacy and security, with Swiss banking secrecy laws dating back to 1934.

The Cayman Islands is one of the world's largest offshore financial centers, with over 100,000 registered companies.

The Basel Accords are international banking regulations that set minimum capital requirements for banks.

The Basel III framework, established in response to the 2008 financial crisis, requires banks to maintain higher capital reserves.

Offshore banking allows individuals and corporations to hold money in jurisdictions with favorable tax and regulatory environments.

ATM AND BRANCH NETWORKS
The total number of ATMs worldwide is approximately 3.5 million, with China having the most at over 1 million.

The world's largest ATM network is operated by the National Financial Switch (NFS) in India with over 250,000 ATMs.

ATMs dispense approximately $1.5 trillion in cash annually worldwide.

The first drive-through ATM was installed in 1971 at a Citibank branch in New York.

Many banks are closing branch locations, with approximately 3,000 branches closing annually in the United States.

Around 60% of all bank branch visits are for transactions that could be performed online or through an ATM.

Some banks are opening "digital branches" that have no tellers and rely entirely on technology and video conferencing.

Huntington Bancshares and its predecessor institutions have operated continuously since 1866, making it one of America's oldest banks.

PAYMENT SYSTEMS
The ACH (Automated Clearing House) system processes over 25 billion transactions annually in the United States.

The Fedwire system, operated by the Federal Reserve, transfers trillions of dollars between banks daily.

Real-time payment systems, such as Zelle and the RTP network, allow instant money transfers 24/7/365.

The Visa network processes over 150 billion transactions annually, making it the largest payment network globally.

Credit card companies charge merchants approximately 2-3% of the purchase amount as processing fees.

Point-of-sale (POS) systems have evolved from mechanical cash registers to sophisticated digital payment terminals.

Contactless payments, including tap-to-pay credit cards and mobile wallets, grew significantly during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Online payment fraud is estimated to cost businesses over $40 billion annually.

REGULATION AND COMPLIANCE
Anti-money laundering (AML) regulations require banks to verify the identity of customers and monitor suspicious transactions.

The Bank Secrecy Act of 1970 was the first major anti-money laundering legislation in the United States.

Know Your Customer (KYC) regulations require banks to obtain identifying information from customers before opening accounts.

Banks spend over $200 billion annually on compliance with financial regulations.

The Patriot Act of 2001 expanded banking regulations in the United States to help prevent terrorist financing.

Bank failures averaged approximately 200 per year during the 1980s Savings and Loan crisis.

The Too Big To Fail concept refers to financial institutions so large that their failure would pose a threat to the entire financial system.

Since 2000, over 500 banks have failed in the United States, with the most failures occurring during the 2008-2010 period.

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) has recovered over $15 billion for consumers since its creation.

INTERESTING BANKING FACTS
The phrase "banker's hours" originated when banks were open only from 10 AM to 3 PM, a tradition that began when bank employees needed time to balance accounts manually.

Banks hire "mystery shoppers" to test customer service quality in their branches.

The largest bank vault in the United States is located at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York and holds over 500,000 gold bars worth approximately $250 billion.

The Bank of America began as the Bank of Italy in 1904, serving San Francisco's immigrant community.

The total value of all gold held in bank vaults worldwide is approximately $12 trillion.

Most bank logos are blue and red because these colors are associated with security, trust, and confidence.

Money derives its value from trust in the issuing government and financial system rather than any intrinsic worth.

The global banking sector employs approximately 20 million people worldwide.

Switzerland's banking secrecy laws were established in the 1930s partly to protect Jewish assets from Nazi confiscation.

During the Great Depression, bank runs caused over 9,000 banks to fail in the United States between 1930 and 1933.

The oldest surviving bank check was found in a collection of documents from ancient Greece and dates to approximately 350 BC.

The first European bank to issue paper money was the Bank of Sweden in 1661.

Banking laws in many countries prohibit commercial banks from owning non-financial businesses.

The world's first bank incorporated by the state was the Bank of Spain, founded in 1782.

Magnetic Ink Character Recognition (MICR), used on checks, was developed in the 1950s by the American Bankers Association.

The credit score system was introduced by FICO in 1989 to standardize credit risk assessment.

Mobile banking fraud increased by over 50% during the COVID-19 pandemic as online banking usage surged.

Blockchain technology, originally developed for cryptocurrencies, is being explored by banks for settlement and clearing.
